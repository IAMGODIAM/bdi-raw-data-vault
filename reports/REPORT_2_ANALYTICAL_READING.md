## I. THE FIRST THING I SEE IS THE FLOOR

I have spent my life reading the data of Black suffering. I have read Du Bois's Philadelphia tables. I have read the Kerner Commission. I have read every edition of the Urban League's *State of Black America* since 1976. I have never seen a dataset that so cleanly isolates what I will call the structural floor — the point below which no index of Black disadvantage has descended in the modern era, regardless of which party held power, which economy was expanding, which moral consensus declared itself victorious.

Look at the unemployment series first. Fifty-four years. 1972 to 2025. In every single year except one — 2020, when white unemployment surged during the first months of a pandemic — Black Americans have faced unemployment at a rate no lower than 1.86 times the white rate. In a year of economic boom. In a year of recession. In the Clinton expansion. In the Obama recovery. In the pre-COVID Trump economy, which was announced as the greatest labor market in Black American history. The floor held. The ratio was 1.856 in 2019.

What this tells a philosopher of race is not primarily about unemployment. It is about the structure of the labor market as a racial institution. Unemployment ratios do not oscillate randomly. They have gravity. The gravity is structural. When the economy expands, Black workers gain jobs. When it contracts, Black workers lose them first and faster. But the ratio of Black to white unemployment does not converge to 1.0. It has a floor. That floor, measured across 54 years of federal data, is approximately 1.86 in all normal economic conditions.

This is not bad luck. This is architecture.

## II. THE BODY DOES NOT LIE

The maternal mortality data is the most morally disturbing series in this dataset, and it must be read in sequence to understand what it says.

In 1930, a Black woman giving birth in the United States died at a rate 1.48 times higher than a white woman. This was during legal Jim Crow. Separate hospitals. Separate wards. Explicit denial of medical access. A healthcare system that did not pretend to serve Black people equally.

In 2022, a Black woman giving birth in the United States died at a rate 2.61 times higher than a white woman. This is after the Civil Rights Act. After Medicare. After the Affordable Care Act. After decades of what the medical establishment called "narrowing disparities."

Let me say this precisely: the ratio of Black to white maternal death has increased from 1.48 to 2.61 over 92 years of modern American medicine. The absolute rate fell dramatically — from 900 per 100,000 in 1930 to 49.5 in 2022. Medicine became more effective at keeping women alive. It became more effective at keeping white women alive. The gap between what medicine does for Black women and what it does for white women has widened.

James Baldwin wrote that the Western world is holding on to its ideas of itself and to the Black people who constitute the bill for those ideas. The maternal mortality data is that bill. In the most intimate, most biological, most irreducible human act — bringing life into the world — the American medical system produces worse outcomes for Black women relative to white women than it did under legal apartheid.

This is what I mean when I say the body does not lie. You can legislate equal protection. You cannot legislate physiology. The body records what the system actually does.

## III. PROPERTY AND THE PERMANENT DISQUALIFICATION

The wealth data requires a specific kind of reading because its surface appearance suggests progress. Black median wealth rose from $12,000 in 1989 to $44,900 in 2022. More than tripled. Surely that is improvement.

Read the denominator. White median wealth rose from $95,000 to $285,000. Also more than tripled. The absolute gap — the actual dollar distance between what the median Black family holds and what the median white family holds — grew from $83,000 in 1989 to $240,100 in 2022. The gap tripled in absolute dollars. The ratio went from 0.126 to 0.158 — an improvement of 3.2 cents per dollar over 33 years.

At that rate of improvement, the wealth ratio would reach parity — 1.0 — in approximately 263 years.

The land data extends this story backward. In 1910, Black Americans owned 15 million acres of farmland. This was 50 years after Emancipation. It is the record of what Black people built when the legal prohibition against building was removed. By 1997, they owned 1.5 million acres. The United States had not confiscated Black land through a single dramatic event. It had confiscated it through a sustained, documented, 87-year process of tax sales, heir property law vulnerabilities, USDA discriminatory lending, harassment, violence, and legal manipulation — each mechanism documented, each outcome measurable, no single moment spectacular enough to fix in public memory.

The philosopher in me notes: this is not the behavior of a society that lost track of Black property. This is the behavior of a society that knew exactly where Black property was and knew exactly how to take it.

## IV. THE CARCERAL WALL

In 1925, Black Americans were incarcerated at 6.45 times the rate of white Americans. In 2022, they were incarcerated at 6.31 times the rate of white Americans. This is after the civil rights movement. After the Voting Rights Act. After Thurgood Marshall. After the NAACP's legal campaign. After the First Step Act. After Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow* became required reading in every progressive institution in America.

The ratio moved 0.14 points in 97 years.

I want to name what this means philosophically. When a system produces a ratio and that ratio holds constant across 97 years — across world wars, across economic cycles, across legal revolutions, across cultural transformations — the ratio is not a byproduct of the system. The ratio is the system's output specification. It is what the system is designed to produce.

This is not a claim about intention. It is a claim about function. We judge systems by what they produce, not by what they intend. What the American criminal justice system has produced, consistently, for nearly a century, is a Black incarceration rate between 5.44 and 7.70 times the white rate. The system is functioning as designed.

The police killing data adds a specific precision. Between 2013 and 2023, 3,496 Black Americans were killed by police. 397 of them were unarmed. Black Americans are 13.6% of the U.S. population and account for approximately 26–29% of those killed by police — an overrepresentation of approximately 2x relative to population share. This has not meaningfully changed in 11 years of data.

The wall is not going up. It is not coming down. It is standing.

## V. THE POLITICAL PARADOX

The voter turnout data contains what I consider the most politically tragic sequence in the entire dataset.

From 1964 to 2007 — 43 years — Black voter turnout never equaled white voter turnout. Black Americans voted in every election despite systematic suppression, despite the long shadow of poll taxes and literacy tests, despite the murders of Medgar Evers and Fannie Lou Hamer and countless others who died for the right to vote. They voted at lower rates than white Americans, but they voted.

In 2008, Black turnout reached 64.7% — within 0.3 points of white turnout. In 2012, it exceeded white turnout for the first and only time in the recorded history of this country: 66.6% to 64.1%. The highest civic participation rate Black Americans have ever achieved.

In 2013, the Supreme Court gutted Section 4(b) of the Voting Rights Act in *Shelby County v. Holder*. By 2016, the Black/white turnout gap had returned to 5.7 points. By 2020, it was 8.3 points — wider than it had been in 2004, before the Obama surge.

The data shows a community that did everything it was told to do. That voted at record rates. That demonstrated its civic commitment in numbers no one could dispute. And a system that, within 12 months, removed the legal mechanism that had protected that vote since 1965.

What Ella Baker understood — and what the voter turnout series confirms — is that a movement that achieves its goals through participation in structures it does not control will find those goals revoked.

## VI. THE COMPOUND TRUTH

Each pillar in this dataset tells a story. But the story the data tells when read together is different from the sum of its parts.

The wealth gap means Black families have less capital to deploy in a crisis. The housing gap means less of that wealth is held in appreciating assets. The mortgage denial gap means access to asset formation is structurally impeded. The incarceration rate means a disproportionate share of Black men are removed from the labor market and from the capacity to build wealth. The educational gap means the pipeline to high-income work is narrowed before it begins. The maternal mortality gap means the physical toll of these conditions is borne disproportionately in the most fundamental biological arena. The voter suppression data means the political mechanism for addressing these conditions is systematically weakened at precisely the moment Black political power peaks.

These are not eight separate problems. They are eight measurement instruments pointed at a single structure.

The question this data does not answer — and which no dataset can answer — is the moral one. What do you do with this? I have spent my life in that question. The data gives you the wound with precision. The response belongs to the living.

What I know is this: the tradition that produced this dataset — from Du Bois's Philadelphia tables to these federal API pulls — is itself an act of resistance. Black people insisting on counting themselves, measuring themselves, documenting themselves. Not waiting for someone else to tell the truth about Black life. Building the instrument of our own evidence.

That is what this dataset is. Evidence. Irrefutable, dual-source verified, CC0-licensed, on-chain sealed evidence. Handle it accordingly.
