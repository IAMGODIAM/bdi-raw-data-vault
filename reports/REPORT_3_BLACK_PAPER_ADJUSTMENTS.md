## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The expanded dataset (v2.0) extends the empirical window from 33 years to a range of 54–122 years depending on pillar, and adds significant historical depth to Pillars 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, and 8. The core 33-year argument (formal equality era) is confirmed and strengthened. The historical extension provides causal architecture that the original dataset could not supply.

**Net result:** The paper is stronger. The argument is deeper. The following adjustments are required — none of them weaken a claim, and several upgrade existing claims from strong to irrefutable.

## ADJUSTMENT 1 — INTRODUCTION: UPGRADE THE SCOPE STATEMENT

**Location:** Introduction, Section "The Data: How This Paper Was Built"
**Current text (approximate):** "The 33-year window was selected to capture the post–Civil Rights Act era of formal legal equality."

**Required adjustment:**
The introduction must be updated to reflect the expanded scope. The 33-year window remains the core empirical argument — but it is now nested inside a longer historical record that provides causation, not just correlation.

**Suggested revision:**
> "The core empirical window of this paper is 1991–2024 — the 33-year era of formal legal equality following the full extension of the Voting Rights Act and Fair Housing Act. This window was selected deliberately: the persistence of the disparities documented here cannot be attributed to explicit legal segregation. They exist within the framework of equal protection. The paper also presents, where federal data permits, historical series extending back to 1900 (health), 1925 (criminal justice), 1940 (housing), 1964 (political), and 1514 (historical architecture). These extended series are not context. They are causation — the documented mechanism that produced the conditions the 33-year window measures."

**Why:** The expanded dataset transforms the paper from a 33-year snapshot into a longitudinal record. The introduction must say so explicitly.

## ADJUSTMENT 3 — CHAPTER 3 (PILLAR 1): ADD THE UNEMPLOYMENT FLOOR FINDING

**Location:** Chapter 3 — Pillar 1: The Wealth Extraction Gap (not yet drafted)
**Status:** This chapter has not been written yet — this is a drafting directive.

**New finding to lead with:**
The BLS 54-year series (1972–2025) establishes what the board is calling the structural floor: Black unemployment has never been below 1.86x white unemployment in any normal economic year. This is the most temporally robust finding in Pillar 1 — 54 years of monthly federal data with no gaps.

**Specific language to include:**
> "The floor is not a floor in the physical sense — it is not a barrier preventing further descent. It is a floor in the architectural sense: the surface upon which every other measure rests. In 54 years of BLS data, the Black/white unemployment ratio has averaged 2.115. It peaked at 2.56 in 1989 — not in a recession year, but in an expansion. The 'historic low' Black unemployment figures announced in 2018 and 2019 — celebrated across the political spectrum — still produced a ratio of 1.867 and 1.856 respectively. Below 1.86, no normal economic year has gone in more than half a century."

## ADJUSTMENT 4 — CHAPTER 4 (PILLAR 2): ADD THE MATERNAL MORTALITY REVERSAL

**Location:** Chapter 4 — Pillar 2: The Body Under Siege (not yet drafted)
**Status:** Drafting directive.

**New critical finding:**
The 1930 vs. 2022 maternal mortality comparison is the most powerful single data point in the expanded dataset. The ratio was **lower in 1930 (1.48x) than in 2022 (2.61x)**. This inverts the expected narrative of medical progress.

**The structure of this section should be:**
1. State the absolute improvement (900 per 100K in 1930 → 49.5 in 2022) — medicine did improve
2. Then pivot: but the gap widened. The ratio went up.
3. Name the mechanism: improvement was differential. Medicine got better for white women faster than for Black women.
4. Anchor with the 2021 peak: 69.9 per 100K — higher than many lower-income nations.

**Specific language:**
> "American medicine made childbirth safer for everyone. It made it safer for white women faster. In 1930 — under legal Jim Crow, in segregated hospitals, with explicit denial of equivalent care — a Black woman died in childbirth 1.48 times more often than a white woman. In 2022, she died 2.61 times more often. The ratio did not narrow. It widened by a factor of 1.76 over 92 years of modern medicine."

## ADJUSTMENT 5 — CHAPTER 4 (PILLAR 2): ADD THE COVID CONVERGENCE REVERSAL

**Location:** Chapter 4 — Pillar 2, life expectancy section
**Finding:** COVID erased a decade of life expectancy convergence in two years.
- 2010: gap was 4.1 years. 2019: gap was 4.0 years. Nine years to close 0.1 years.
- 2020: gap jumped to 5.8 years. 2021: 5.6 years. Two years to reverse a decade.
- 2022: 3.7 years (recovery beginning).

**Language for the chapter:**
> "COVID did not create the life expectancy gap. It revealed its depth. Nine years of slow convergence — from 4.1 years in 2010 to 4.0 years in 2019 — were erased in two. The gap widened by 1.8 years between 2019 and 2020 alone. A crisis stress-tests the resilience of a system. The 2020–2021 data documents what happens to Black lives when the system is stressed: the gap that had taken a decade to close by 0.1 years opens by 1.8 years in twelve months."

## ADJUSTMENT 6 — CHAPTER 7 (PILLAR 5): STRENGTHEN THE HOUSING HEADLINE

**Location:** Chapter 7 — Pillar 5: The Housing Architecture of Exclusion (not yet drafted)
**New finding from expanded dataset:**

The decennial homeownership series extends the housing gap back to 1940. This produces the paper's most important housing finding:

**The homeownership gap was 22.1 percentage points in 1940. It is 28.9 percentage points in 2022.**

The Fair Housing Act was signed in 1968 when the gap was approximately 24 percentage points. The gap today is wider than when the law was signed.

**Chapter 7 should open with this finding.** It is the housing equivalent of the maternal mortality reversal — a quantified indictment of the gap between legal remedy and structural outcome.

**Language:**
> "The Fair Housing Act of 1968 made housing discrimination illegal. In 1968, the gap between Black and white homeownership was approximately 24 percentage points. In 2022, it was 28.9 percentage points. The law did not close the gap. The gap grew by nearly 5 percentage points in the 54 years following the law's passage."

## ADJUSTMENT 7 — CHAPTER 8 (PILLAR 6): ADD THE SHELBY COUNTY INFLECTION

**Location:** Chapter 8 — Pillar 6: Political Power and Its Limits (not yet drafted)
**New finding:** The voter turnout data tells the sharpest inflection story in the dataset.

2012: Black turnout 66.6% — exceeded white turnout for the first and only time on record.
2013: *Shelby County v. Holder* — VRA Section 4(b) gutted.
2016: Black/white turnout gap: −5.7 points.
2020: Gap: −8.3 points.

The inflection is documented, timestamped, and causally proximate. The Supreme Court decision came between the peak and the decline.

**Language:**
> "The data contains one moment of achievement: 2012. Black voter turnout at 66.6% exceeded white voter turnout at 64.1% — the only time in 56 years of recorded CPS data that this occurred. One year later, the Supreme Court removed Section 4(b) of the Voting Rights Act in Shelby County v. Holder. By 2016, the gap was −5.7 points. By 2020, −8.3 points. The peak was 2012. The decision was 2013. The data does not establish causation — that is a legal argument. But the timing is documented."

## ADJUSTMENT 8 — CHAPTER 10 (PILLAR 8): ANCHOR WITH THREE SPECIFIC FIGURES

**Location:** Chapter 10 — Pillar 8: The Historical Architecture (not yet drafted)
**Three numbers that must appear:**

1. **12,521,337** — the number of Africans forcibly transported in the transatlantic slave trade (Slave Voyages Database, 2022 edition). Not a round number. The precision matters. The precision is the point.

2. **1,800,000** — the number who died in transit, before reaching the Americas. These are lives that appear in no count of the enslaved population because they never arrived. They are in the data only as an absence.

3. **13,500,000 acres** — the net loss of Black-owned farmland from 1910 to 1997. 90% of the peak. This is the largest systematic asset transfer in American history without a conventional name.

These three numbers go into Chapter 10 as anchors. The historical chapter should build from them.
